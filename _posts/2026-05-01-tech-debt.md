There's a moment every software engineer knows.
You open a ticket.
The change looks simple, maybe just a few hours of work.
You open the relevant source code, start reading, and 20 minutes later you're somewhere completely different in the codebase, unsure how you got there, trying to understand why touching THIS thing requires touching THAT thing, which requires touching 10+ other things, which somehow lead you back to where you started.

Hours becomes a day.
The day becomes two.
Your manager doesn't understand why a "small feature" can take a week.
You don't quite know how to explain it either.
The code technically works.
But something in the system has quietly made every change harder and more expensive than it should be.

That quiet, compounding cost has a name: **technical debt**.

The Loan You Didn't Know You Were Taking
----------------------------------------

The metaphor was coined by Ward Cunningham, and it's right: technical debt works like a financial loan.

When your team cuts corners to ship faster, by skipping documentation, writing quick-and-dirty solutions, or not cleaning up a messy area of code, you're taking out a loan. You get speed now. But you pay interest later, on every future change that has to navigate the mess you left behind.

The treacherous part is how invisible it is.
Unlike a bank loan, nobody signs anything.
Nobody knows the exact balance.
Interest accrues silently, in slowed delivery, in engineers spending hours on context that should only take minutes, in bugs that keep appearing in the same places.
By the time it becomes visible, usually when the team starts missing deadlines they can't explain, the debt has been compounding for months or years.

How I Watched It Happen in Real Time
------------------------------------

I spent 8 years at [MyScript](https://www.myscript.com), a handwriting recognition software company.
For the first few years, the team was small, the codebase was young, and we moved fast.
Fast was the right call.
We were building products, proving concepts, shipping to enterprise clients.
Some corners were cut deliberately.
Others, honestly, because we didn't know better yet.

A few years later, something had quietly changed.

Every release came with regressions, with bugs appearing in features we hadn't even touched.
We'd ship an improvement to a module and discover that the functionalities in a completely unrelated module had broken.
We'd investigate, find the cause buried in code that connected things with no business being connected, fix it, and ship again.
Then the next release would bring different regressions.

At one point, a major feature delivery was delayed not because the feature was hard to build, but because we were spending more time investigating regressions than actually building.
The symptoms were obvious.
The root cause was harder to name.

What had happened, I eventually understood, was this: over years of fast decisions made in isolation, the pieces of the codebase had become entangled.
**Business logic**, *the rules of what the software does*, had become mixed together with **infrastructure concerns**, *the plumbing of how it does it*.
Objects from different parts of the system were reaching directly into each other's internals.
Changing one thing rippled unpredictably into a batch of others.

We hadn't taken one big loan.
We'd taken dozens of tiny ones over years.
And now the interest was due.

The Ways Debt Accumulates
-------------------------

Looking back, and having seen similar patterns at other companies since, the causes fall into a recognizable pattern.

**The organizational ones.**
Business pressure is the most common: features rolled out before they're truly finished, patches hiding unfinished work.
Nobody meant to create a problem; everyone was trying to ship.
Closely related is what I'd call the *understanding gap*: managers approve shortcuts without realizing that this sprint's shortcut becomes next quarter's slowdown.
And when there are no shared standards (no agreed way of doing things, no regular code reviews), each engineer's individual choices accumulate into a fractured system where no part quite speaks the same language as any other.

**The technical ones.**
Systems that grow without clear internal boundaries (where everything is allowed to touch everything) gradually become what engineers call a [*Big Ball of Mud*](https://deviq.com/antipatterns/big-ball-of-mud/).
No one designed it to be unmaintainable; it evolved there through accumulated convenience.
The absence of automated tests is a particular accelerant: without a safety net that catches regressions immediately, teams become conservative and slow, afraid to change anything for fear of breaking something invisible.
And when code is never cleaned up even as requirements change (because "we'll get to it later"), the mismatch between what the code assumes and what the business needs quietly widens.

**The human ones.**
When knowledge lives only in people's heads and never gets written down, it leaves with those people.
When teams don't communicate (working in silos, making conflicting decisions without realizing it), the codebase fragments.
And sometimes, honestly, the problem is simply that someone didn't yet know how to do it better.
That's not a character flaw; it's a training and mentorship gap.

Paying It Back and Not Making It Worse
--------------------------------------

At [MyScript](https://www.myscript.com), the way out wasn't to stop everything and rewrite from scratch.
That approach almost never works: you'd stop delivering value for months and likely recreate the same problems with slightly different names.

Instead, we tackled it incrementally.
We restructured the architecture to give each part of the system an explicit boundary, a clear space it owned and was responsible for, with defined ways to communicate with other parts rather than reaching in directly.
We did it gradually, one domain at a time, alongside regular feature work.
No delivery pause.
No heroic rewrite sprint.

The results were visible within a few months.
Regression rates dropped.
Engineers started making changes with more confidence.
Pull requests, the packages of change engineers submit for review, became more focused, less defensive.
The team had been operating in a state of low-grade anxiety about touching anything; that started to lift.

Later, consulting at [PERF](https://www.perf-solutions.com), an industrial digitalization software company serving nuclear, construction, and manufacturing operations, I found similar entanglement on the backend side.
Business rules were mixed together with database calls and infrastructure plumbing in the same place.
Adding a new feature meant navigating all of it, touching things that shouldn't need touching.
The remedy followed the same principle: [*Separation of Concerns*](https://deviq.com/principles/separation-of-concerns/).
Business logic on one side, infrastructure on the other, with a clean boundary between, so new features could be added without unintended ripples.

In both cases, the cleanup wasn't magic.
It was simply paying back what had been borrowed, with interest.

Habits That Keep the Balance Low
--------------------------------

Prevention is cheaper than repayment.
The teams I've seen handle debt well tend to share the following principles:

**They test as they build.**
Automated tests aren't just about catching bugs, they force you to write code that can be tested in isolation, which almost always means code that's more cleanly separated.
The test suite also acts as a safety net that lets the team move faster, not slower: you can make a change and immediately know if something broke, instead of discovering it weeks later in production.

**They make decisions explicit.**
When a shortcut is taken consciously, because shipping by Friday matters more than perfect architecture today, it gets written down: what was decided, why, and the intent to revisit it.
Invisible debt is the dangerous kind.
Tracked debt is manageable.

**They clean a little with every change.**
There's an old camping principle: leave the campsite better than you found it.
The same instinct can be applied to a codebase: every time you're in an area, leave it slightly cleaner than you found it.
It prevents the slow accumulation of broken windows.
Not a rewrite.
Just a little better, every time.

Should You Avoid All Technical Debt?
------------------------------------

*No*, and anyone who tells you otherwise hasn't shipped real software under real constraints.

The banking metaphor is deliberate: *sometimes taking a loan is the right move.*
A team that refactors obsessively instead of shipping is just as stuck as a team drowning in debt.

The startup that polishes code to perfection before anyone uses it has no customers.
The consultant who wants months to restructure the architecture before delivering anything has no contract.

The goal isn't zero debt.
It's *intentional* debt: shortcuts you take with open eyes, track explicitly, and repay before the interest compounds into something structural.

The debt that destroys teams isn't the kind that was chosen.
It's the kind that accumulated silently, that nobody noticed until the system had become too tangled to change without breaking something, that grew from dozens of small decisions nobody thought twice about.

The difference, in the end, is ***awareness***.
Know what you're borrowing.
Know what you owe.
And keep paying it down, a little at a time, before the interest owns you.
